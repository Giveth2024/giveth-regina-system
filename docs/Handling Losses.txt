This is actually a different business event from a sale, and it's good that you're thinking about it now. I **would not** record it through the sales endpoint.

A sale means:

> The business earned money by selling inventory.

Spoilage means:

> The business lost inventory without making a sale.

Those are fundamentally different events.

---

## I would create a separate inventory adjustment / loss system

For example:

```text
POST /api/giveth/losses/add
```

or, even more generally:

```text
POST /api/giveth/stock/adjust
```

with an adjustment type.

## A losses table

For example:

```text
losses
-------
id
user_id
stock_id
quantity
cost_price
total_loss
reason
notes
created_at
```

Example row:

| stock   | qty |   cost | total_loss | reason  |
| ------- | --: | -----: | ---------: | ------- |
| Sausage |  24 | 395.83 |    9500.00 | Spoiled |

Notice I would record the **cost price**, not the selling price, because the business lost what it paid for the stock. If you also want to estimate missed revenue, you can calculate that separately.

---

## What happens?

When recording a loss:

1. Validate that stock exists.
2. Ensure there are at least 24 sausages in stock.
3. Reduce stock by 24.
4. Insert a row into `losses`.
5. Commit the transaction.

---

## Will it affect the balance?

Yes, but **indirectly**.

Think of it like this:

### Sales

Increase revenue.

```
Revenue +17,500
```

---

### Expenses

Decrease cash.

```
Bought cooking oil
-50,000
```

---

### Losses

Decrease inventory value.

```
24 sausages spoiled
Inventory -9,500
```

No cash leaves the till that day, but the business has lost value because inventory that was purchased can no longer be sold.

## Should it have its own endpoint?

Yes.

I would keep these operations separate because they represent different business actions:

```
POST /sales/add
```

A customer bought something.

```
POST /expenses/add
```

The business spent money.

```
POST /losses/add
```

The business lost inventory.

```
POST /stock/add
```

New inventory arrived.

---

### My recommendation

Rather than creating a table that's only for losses, I'd consider a more general **`stock_adjustments`** table. It can record any manual change to inventory:

| adjustment_type      | Example                        |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| `Loss`               | Spoiled sausages               |
| `Damage`             | Broken bottle                  |
| `Theft`              | Missing inventory              |
| `Correction`         | Fixed an incorrect stock count |
| `Donation`           | Gave food away                 |
| `Return to Supplier` | Sent damaged goods back        |

Then every adjustment records:

* who made it (`user_id`)
* which stock item (`stock_id`)
* quantity changed
* reason/type
* notes
* timestamp

Your stock is updated accordingly, and your reports can filter by adjustment type. It's more flexible than a dedicated `losses` table and will likely cover future inventory scenarios without needing another schema redesign.
